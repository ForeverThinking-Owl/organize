# Tools, People & Organizations — The First Principles of Practice

---

## I. The Fundamental Problem of Practice: Availability

Life exists in a world that requires continuous transformation. In thermodynamic terms, the world tends toward diffusion, dissipation, and homogenization. Without sustained energy input, structural maintenance, and differentiation, any system will eventually reach an undifferentiated equilibrium. For life, this equilibrium means the **disappearance of availability**.

As open systems, living things must constantly absorb energy, matter, and information from their environment. Through metabolism, excretion, movement, perception, selection, and feedback, they transform external objects into structures they can actually use.

> Consider a cow eating grass. Grass does not exist in a form directly absorbable as protein. Ruminants depend on microbes in the rumen to ferment feed, breaking down plant fiber into usable forms of energy and nutrition. The cow's life depends on a transformation structure composed of grass, rumen, microbes, fermentation, and the digestive system working together.

This example reveals something fundamental: the core of life activity is **transforming the relationship between the environment and the self**.

**Practice is the process by which life, in order to maintain or expand its ordered structure, employs some intermediary to transform objects not yet directly available in the environment into something usable.**

"Usable" here includes: usable energy, matter, information, pathways, tools, memory, and collaboration. Chaos itself is not the core problem — the core problem is the loss of availability. Life cannot make the entire world ordered, but it can continuously produce availability in local contexts.

> **The first-principle meaning of practice is the production of availability.**

## II. Tools: Stabilized Forms of Transformation

In humans, transformation acquired new forms. Humans rely on bodily structures, physiological mechanisms, and symbiotic systems to transform the environment — and they also externalize certain transformation methods as tools:

- **Stone tools** stabilized cutting, striking, and peeling
- **Fire** stabilized heating, lighting, defense, and gathering
- **Language** stabilized the transmission of experience
- **Writing** stabilized memory
- **Ledgers** stabilized accounting and accountability
- **Machines** stabilized physical force
- **Computers** stabilized information processing

**A tool is an externalized, stabilized, repeatably executable form of transformation.**

A stone becomes a tool only when it enters the practice of cutting, striking, or making. An API becomes a tool only when it enters the practice of order processing, inventory scheduling, customer service, or financial accounting. Tools acquire meaning through practice. They stabilize a repeatable form of transformation within that practice.

The importance of early stone tools lies not in their physical form but in their externalization of practices — cutting, striking, peeling, shaping — making these practices repeatable, learnable, transmissible, and improvable. Tools changed the form of practice. Life relies on bodily structures and immediate behavior to transform the environment; tools allow transformation methods to detach from the present body and become external forms that can be preserved, taught, combined, and extended.

In modern systems:

| Tool Type | Becomes a Tool Only When |
|-----------|-------------------------|
| API | It enters a social production practice |
| RPA | It enters the practice of legacy system data entry, query, submission, and write-back |
| MCP Tool | It enters an action chain constrained by rules, objects, and responsibility |
| Software Interface | It enters a human judgment and operation workflow |
| Automation Script | It enters a repeatable, auditable production chain |

> **The essence of a tool is a stabilized form of transformation.**

## III. Skill: The Orchestration of Tools

Tools alone do not constitute skill.

- Knowing how to hold a knife still leaves a **distance of practice** from knowing how to cook
- Knowing how to open a system still leaves a **distance of judgment** from handling a customer complaint
- Knowing how to call an API still leaves a **distance of responsibility** from completing a refund review
- Knowing how to trigger automation still leaves a **distance of coordination** from completing a production practice

Skill rises above a list of tools. It manifests as a person's mastery, selection, combination, and judgment-based use of tools.

> **Skill is the orchestration of tools.**

A chef's skill lies in knowing when to use the knife, how to use fire, how to handle ingredients, and how to control sequence and timing. A doctor's skill lies in judging — within a specific clinical context — how to use inquiry, examination, imaging, and medication. An engineer's skill lies in orchestrating code repositories, logging systems, monitoring dashboards, and deployment systems into a practice process of diagnosis, modification, testing, and release.

Tools externalize practice methods; skill re-internalizes tools as human practice capability. Tools allow a form of transformation to be preserved; skill allows that form of transformation to be borne by a person. Tools are executable practice methods; skill is the organized use of those methods.

This also explains the difference between tool invocation and practice. A system can call many interfaces, but if it lacks judgment about **object, purpose, sequence, boundary, responsibility, and feedback**, it is merely triggering actions. Skill requires:

- **Order** between tools
- **Purpose** behind tool use
- **Judgment** in tool selection
- **Feedback** from tool results

## IV. People: Bearers of Skill, Judgment & Responsibility

People are the bearers of skill, judgment, purpose, and responsibility.

People do not appear abstractly in practice — they appear with a particular structure of skills. Some excel at making, some at caring, some at calculating, some at communicating, some at judging risk, some at repairing systems, some at designing rules, some at coordinating others. A person's practice capability comes both from mastery of tools and from judgment of context.

The same knife can be used for cooking, for repair, or for harm. **A tool cannot, by itself, determine the purpose of practice.** Tools must be placed within a structure of purpose, rules, boundaries, and responsibility. The meaning of a person lies in connecting tools, skills, purpose, and responsibility together.

A person in practice bears at least three things:

1. **Judging purpose**: why this should be done
2. **Choosing method**: which tools, in what sequence, within what boundaries
3. **Bearing consequences**: if the result is wrong, transgresses boundaries, fails, or harms others — where does responsibility fall

> A model can invoke tools. A script can execute steps. A system can write data. But purpose, object, rules, responsibility, and feedback determine whether it enters practice. People place tools, skills, and responsibility into a single structure, giving practice direction and consequence.

## V. Collaboration: When Complex Practice Exceeds Individual Skill Boundaries

When a practice requires more than a single person's skill boundary, different skills must collaborate.

- Hunting requires observation, tracking, tool-making, encirclement, and distribution
- Medicine requires diagnosis, nursing, testing, surgery, pharmacy, and administration
- Manufacturing requires design, procurement, processing, quality inspection, warehousing, and delivery
- Customer service requires communication, inquiry, judgment, after-sales, finance, technical support, and feedback

Sufficiently complex practices all require multiple skills to form collaboration around a shared object.

Collaboration means different skills entering into relationship around a common object. Someone is responsible for identifying the object, someone for processing it, someone for providing tools, someone for judging risk, someone for executing writes, someone for feeding back results, someone for correcting rules.

> Complex practice is complex not because of the number of steps, but because different skills must be arranged into a **sustainable relationship**.

Here, stable structures begin to emerge. Complex practice demands that different skills collaborate within rules and responsibilities — and once collaboration stabilizes, it forms a higher-order practice structure.

---

## VI. Organization: The Stable Structure of Collaboration

**An organization is the stable collaborative structure formed by human skills around complex practice needs.**

- A hunting party is a collaborative structure of observation, tracking, tool-making, encirclement, and distribution skills
- A hospital is a collaborative structure of diagnosis, nursing, testing, surgery, pharmacy, and administration skills
- A factory is a collaborative structure of design, procurement, manufacturing, quality inspection, warehousing, and sales skills
- An enterprise is a stable structure formed by sales, customer service, finance, R&D, operations, and delivery skills around social production needs

The meaning of organization lies in practice. Organizations form to continuously satisfy some social practice need. They preserve tools, distribute skills, define roles, enforce boundaries, establish responsibility, accumulate memory, and correct themselves through feedback.

An organization contains at least six elements:

| Element | Function |
|---------|----------|
| **Object** | Provides practice direction |
| **Skill** | Provides action capability |
| **Tool** | Provides transformation methods |
| **Rule** | Defines boundaries |
| **Responsibility** | Bears consequences |
| **Memory** | Preserves experience and corrects the future |

Thus, practice is the primary concept. Tools are the externalization of practice methods. Skill is the orchestration of tools. Collaboration is multiple skills jointly responding to complex practice. Organization is the stable structure of collaboration.

> **An organization is the form that stably orchestrates people, skills, tools, rules, responsibility, and memory around the need to satisfy complex practice.**

## VII. AI: A Change in How Practice Is Orchestrated

AI is a new variable in this chain. Its significance lies in **changing the orchestration relationship between tools, skills, and collaboration**.

Previously, tools were primarily selected and operated by human hands. People identified objects, chose tools, combined steps, judged results, and drove the next step. Software systems mostly waited for human input. Traditional RPA mostly simulated human clicks and data entry within fixed scripts.

AI — especially large language models — brings tools into a new phase. It can understand language, identify objects, interpret context, select tools, combine steps, invoke systems, read feedback, and request governance when necessary. The selection, combination, and execution of tools can begin to be driven by language, context, and rules. Parts of skill structures can begin to be formalized. Parts of collaboration scheduling can begin to be automated.

**The significance of AI lies in changing how practice is scheduled.** It enables the 5W1H of practice to be handled:

| Dimension | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|
| **When** | When to start, pause, escalate, or terminate |
| **Where** | In which system, scene, unit, or context it occurs |
| **What** | What object and problem this actually is |
| **Why** | Why this action is needed in this context |
| **Who** | Which role, which skill, which collaborative structure bears it |
| **How** | Which tools to invoke, in what order, within what boundaries |

**AI acquires practice scheduling authority. Practice sovereignty remains with people and organizations.**

Practice sovereignty includes: purpose, boundary, risk, exception, and responsibility. People decide what goals are worth pursuing, what boundaries must be maintained, what risks must be confirmed, what exceptions require adjudication, and what results require accountability.

The human position thus shifts upward:

| Past (Executor) | Future (Governor) |
|-----------------|-------------------|
| Query-taker | Goal-setter |
| Data-entry clerk | Boundary-definer |
| Reminder-chaser | Exception-adjudicator |
| Copy-paster | Risk-approver |
| Executor | Rule-corrector |
| — | Ultimate responsibility-bearer |

## VIII. From Collaboration to Autonomous Operation

When tool executability rises, skill formalization deepens, and collaboration scheduling can be driven by language, context, and rules — organizations can move toward a new form: **the autonomously operating organization**.

"Autonomous operation" here points to a practice structure in which an organization — around some practice need — automatically orchestrates tools, skills, and people, and continuously operates within governance and feedback. It still contains human purpose, boundaries, responsibility, and judgment; at the same time, the scheduling and execution of routine practice is progressively handed over to an operating structure composed of tools, skills, and AI.

The basic structure of this organizational form can be expressed as:

```
Practice need → Tools
Tool orchestration → Skill
Skill bearing → People
Human skill collaboration → Organization
Organization operation → Social practice
Practice feedback → New tools, new skills, new collaboration
```

Within this structure, AI participates in practice scheduling. It helps judge when to start practice, where it takes place, what object to handle, why to act this way, who bears the practice, and how to combine tools to complete it. People retain practice sovereignty: defining goals, setting boundaries, approving risks, handling exceptions, bearing responsibility, and correcting the rules for the next round of practice.

> **An autonomously operating organization is the operational form in which human skills, tools, and judgment are re-orchestrated so the organization can continuously satisfy complex practice needs.**

The questions truly worth discussing in the AI era:

- How tools are orchestrated into skills
- How skills are borne by people
- How people's skills are organized
- How organizations meet the needs of social practice
- How AI participates in practice scheduling
- How people retain practice sovereignty

---

## IX. Conclusion: Tools, People & Organizations

The world naturally tends toward dissipation. Life must maintain availability.

| Concept | Essence |
|---------|---------|
| **Practice** | Transforming what is not yet directly available into what is usable |
| **Tool** | An externalized, stabilized, repeatably executable form of transformation |
| **Skill** | A person's mastery, selection, combination, and judgment-based use of tools |
| **Collaboration** | Multiple skills jointly responding to complex practice needs |
| **Organization** | The stable structure of collaboration |

Tools are executable practice methods. Skill is the orchestration of tools. People are the bearers of skill, judgment, and responsibility. Organization is the collaborative structure formed by human skills around social practice needs.

The significance of AI lies in changing how tools, skills, and collaboration are orchestrated. It enables the 5W1H of practice to be scheduled by language, context, and rules, allowing more routine practice to operate autonomously.

> **AI acquires practice scheduling authority. People retain practice sovereignty.** The purpose, boundary, exception, and responsibility of practice must still be borne by people and organizations.

Ultimately, what society needs is an operating structure that can organize tools, skills, and people around the needs of practice.

**This is the first principle of tools, people, and organizations.**
